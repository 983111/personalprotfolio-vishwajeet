Self-Verification Prompting for Reliable Chatbots

Author: Vishwajeet Adkine | AI Reliability & Safety Published: November 04, 2025

Abstract

This paper introduces Self-Verification Prompting, a novel technique designed to mitigate error accumulation and enhance the reliability of multi-step reasoning in Large Language Models (LLMs) that employ Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting. Inspired by the human cognitive process of double-checking one's work, the method equips LLMs with an internal mechanism to verify the consistency of their own generated conclusions against the original problem constraints.

Methodology

Self-Verification operates in two distinct stages:

1. Forward Reasoning

The LLM is initially prompted to generate multiple candidate solutions and corresponding CoT reasoning paths. This generates a pool of potential answers, some correct and some potentially flawed.

2. Backward Verification (Condition Mask Verification)

Each candidate conclusion is rigorously tested for its internal consistency. This stage utilizes Condition Mask Verification, where the problem, along with a candidate answer, is reformulated into a declarative statement. A key condition from the original input (e.g., an initial quantity or number) is then masked and replaced with a variable (e.g., 'X'). The LLM is tasked to predict the value of this masked condition using the generated conclusion and the remaining information.

An Interpretable Verification Score is computed based on the frequency with which the LLM correctly predicts the original, masked condition across multiple independent verification runs. The core hypothesis is that a correct answer will be mathematically and logically consistent, enabling the model to reconstruct the original premise, while an incorrect answer will lead to an inconsistent or "hallucinatory" reconstruction.

Key Findings and Conclusion

The implementation of Self-Verification Prompting demonstrated significant improvements in reasoning accuracy across diverse tasks, including arithmetic, commonsense, and logical reasoning datasets. The results confirmed the hypothesis that LLMs possess an emergent self-verification capability. By automatically verifying candidate answers and selecting the one with the highest consistency score, the method effectively reduces sensitivity to individual mistakes within the CoT, thereby enhancing the overall accuracy and dependability of LLM-based reasoning systems without requiring external verifiers or extensive fine-tuning.
